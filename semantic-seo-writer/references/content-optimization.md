# Content Optimization Rules (Rules 1-20)

Detailed explanations and examples for the core content writing and optimization rules.

---

## Rule 1: 40-Word Featured Snippet

Stick to a 40-word limit per answer where possible to help trigger snippets.

**Example:**

Question: "What are antioxidants?"

Answer: "Antioxidants are compounds that prevent cell damage by neutralizing free radicals, supporting overall health."

**Why it works:** Short, direct answers increase the likelihood of appearing as featured snippets, especially for definitions or quick responses, enhancing visibility on search engines.

---

## Rule 2: Answer Boolean Questions

Boolean Questions should start with Yes or No.

**Example:**

Does Water In Food Help When Drinking Water Is Restricted?

Yes, eating food high in water content that is easily digestible helps with overall fluid intake when drinking water is restricted.

According to Wiseman, digesting fat is the hardest and requires a lot of water.

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) recommends not eating salty foods as they can increase thirst.

## Rule 4: Author Rules

- Avoid starting sentences with "if".
- Minimize the use of the word "also".
- Present statements before conditions.
- Avoid nested statements.
- Measure the vocabulary gap between queries and documents.

---

## Rule 5: Avoid Analogies

Don't Use Analogies. An analogy is a way of explaining something by comparing it to something else that is similar. It's like saying one thing is similar to another to help people understand a concept better.

**Examples:**

Instead of: "Coffee is like fuel for your body."
Write: "Coffee provides energy through caffeine."

Instead of: "The internet is a treasure trove of knowledge."
Write: "The internet contains vast amounts of information on various topics."

Instead of: "Success is a ladder, and every step brings you closer to the top."
Write: "Success is achieved through consistent effort and progress toward specific goals."

Instead of: "A healthy diet is the backbone of a strong immune system."
Write: "A healthy diet supports the immune system by providing essential nutrients."

Instead of: "This software is a Swiss Army knife for productivity."
Write: "This software offers a variety of tools to help improve productivity."

---

## Rule 6: Avoid Confusing Users and Bots

Do not send the reader, or the text processors to the back of the article.

Do not send the reader, or the text processors to the back of the article by saying "As stated before", or "As it is explained in the Y section".

**Wrong Structure:** "As it is shown in the article, the most dangerous poisons in the world have been determined by the P method."

**Correct Structure:** "The most dangerous poisons in the world have been determined by the P Method."

**Explanation:** Instead of directing readers back to a previous section (e.g., "As stated before"), restate the information directly. This keeps the content flow uninterrupted and makes it easier for readers to follow without needing to recall previous sections.

---

## Rule 7: Avoid Coreference Errors

The Importance of Clear Pronoun Usage in Content. No doubt, using pronouns can make your copy short and reader-friendly.

But improper pronoun usage in a sentence may result in a "coreference error."

This error happens when search engines struggle to understand which noun or entity the pronoun is referring to.

**Example of a Coreference Error:**

"Joe Rogan did a podcast with Elon Musk, and he shared his thoughts on finance and law." In this case, it's unclear who shared their thoughts — Joe Rogan or Elon Musk?

Since the pronoun "he" doesn't clearly refer to a specific entity, Google may get confused while processing the sentence.

**How Google Handles It:** Google will attempt to resolve this ambiguity by searching for co-occurrences of both entities on the web.

However, such confusion signals to Google that the article may not be well-structured or carefully written.

**The Takeaway:**

- Your article should always have a clear structure.
- Every entity should be clearly specified within its context. By following this, you ensure better readability for both users and search engines.

## Rule 8: Avoid Everyday Language in Articles

Never use "everyday language" in the articles. "Everyday language" (also called conversational language) is the type of language people typically use in casual, informal interactions. It includes:

- **Slang:** Words or phrases used informally in specific groups (e.g., "cool," "lit," "hang out").
- **Colloquialisms:** Expressions that are region-specific or culturally common but informal (e.g., "gonna" for "going to").
- **Contractions:** Shortened forms of words (e.g., "can't," "won't," "didn't").
- **Simple phrases or idioms:** Phrases that rely on common understanding or familiarity (e.g., "piece of cake" for something easy).
- **Pronouns:** Informal, conversational pronouns like "you guys" or "everybody."
- **Phrasal verbs:** Informal verbs that combine with particles (e.g., "pick up," "turn down," "look after").

**Formal and Professional Diction Examples:**

| Everyday Language | Formal Language |
|---|---|
| "This phone is pretty cool." | "This device offers a range of innovative features." |
| "The company's growth has been super impressive this year." | "The company has demonstrated substantial growth this fiscal year." |
| "This strategy will help you avoid making the same mistakes again." | "This strategy is designed to minimize the likelihood of recurring errors." |

## Rule 9: Avoid Linking to Citations

Don't link out to citations while writing content.

**Good Example (follow this):** Content that mentions research without linking to it externally, like svalbardi.com/blogs/water/benefit — there is no link to citations.

**Bad Example (avoid this):** Content like healthline.com that links throughout the article to scientific research and lists sources at the bottom. We will not do that in order to not pass link juice.

We will also not mention related sources at the bottom of the content.

## Rule 10: Avoid Product Promotion in Informational Articles

Do not promote products while giving information about the topic.

**Example:** In an article about coffee health benefits, avoid saying "buy our organic coffee for best results."

**Explanation:** Keep informational pages unbiased, focusing purely on the topic, which makes them more trustworthy.

## Rule 11: Avoid Uncertain Words / Semantic Triples

### Sentence Structure: Subject, Predicate & Object

A sentence is divided into three parts: Subject, Predicate, and Object.

- **Subject:** The subject of a sentence is a person, place, thing, or idea. It is always doing or being something.
- **Predicate:** The predicate provides information about the subject. It includes the verb and any other words that modify or describe the action.
- **Object:** The object is the person or thing upon whom or upon which the action of the verb is carried out.

### Writing Using Semantic Triples

Instead of writing a plain sentence, you can structure your information using semantic triples.

**Example:**

Before: "Harry Potter is a popular book series written by J.K. Rowling."
After (using a semantic triple): "Harry Potter written by J.K. Rowling is a popular book series."

Using semantic triples to win a Featured Snippet will enhance your chances of success.

**How It Works:**

Before: "The Eiffel Tower is located in Paris and was designed by Gustave Eiffel."
After: "The Eiffel Tower, designed by Gustave Eiffel, is located in Paris."

**Breakdown of the Revised Sentence:**
- **Main Thing:** "The Eiffel Tower" — What we are talking about.
- **Special Fact About It:** "designed by Gustave Eiffel" — An important detail or feature.
- **Main Information:** "is located in Paris" — The most important or final piece of information.

This method connects important details directly to the main topic, making the sentence clearer and more focused.

## Rule 12: Avoid Unnecessary Sentences

Don't create an extra sentence if there is no logical reason.

**Example:**

Question: How old is Tanjiro in Demon Slayer anime?

- Answer 1: Tanjiro is 25 years old. His birthdate is Dec 2, 2002.
- Answer 2: Tanjiro is 25 years old and his birthdate is Dec 2, 2002.
- Answer 3: Tanjiro is 25 years old. Tanjiro's birthdate is Dec 2, 2002.

**Answer 2 is correct**, because with each new sentence search engines will use a new token. Both Answer 1 and Answer 3 will require two tokens, but Answer 2 will require only 1 token.

## Rule 13: Be Certain

"Sun will rise tomorrow" — A limit on how certain we can be because it depends on time and uses the wrong form. (It's just a possibility)

"Sun raises every day." — A fact.

Knowledge should be certain and definite.

## Rule 14: Be Specific When Describing Things

Do not tell "The symptoms of X disease...". Tell, "There are 6 severe symptoms of X disease, these are... There are 9 rare symptoms of X disease..."

Experts are specific.

### General vs. Expert-Like Examples

**Condition Explanation:**
- General: "The symptoms of malaria include fever, chills, and nausea."
- Expert-Like: "Malaria has 4 primary symptoms, which are fever, chills, headache, and nausea. Additionally, there are 3 rare symptoms: confusion, seizures, and bleeding."

**Product Benefits:**
- General: "The benefits of Product Y are numerous."
- Expert-Like: "Product Y offers 5 main benefits: 1) improved battery life, 2) faster processing speed, 3) enhanced camera quality, 4) increased durability, and 5) waterproofing. Two additional features, though less common, include facial recognition and voice control."

**Technical Description:**
- General: "The properties of Material Z include high tensile strength and durability."
- Expert-Like: "Material Z has 3 core properties: 1) high tensile strength, 2) excellent thermal resistance, and 3) remarkable durability. Two lesser-known properties include corrosion resistance and flexibility at low temperatures."

**Medical Treatment Effects:**
- General: "Side effects of Treatment A are varied."
- Expert-Like: "Treatment A has 4 common side effects, including nausea, dizziness, fatigue, and dry mouth. Additionally, there are 2 rare side effects: liver inflammation and blood pressure fluctuations."

## Rule 15: Understand Context of Verbs

"Increase" signals "health". "Improve" signals "skill" + "health". "Develop" signals "skill".

Determine predicates wisely:

- **Increase** for measurable health metrics, quantities, or intensities. Example: "Consistent stretching can increase flexibility over time."
- **Improve** for skills or health outcomes that can be enhanced. Example: "Practicing regularly will improve your language proficiency."
- **Develop** for gradual skill acquisition or growth in processes. Example: "Working in a startup environment helps employees develop resilience and adaptability."

## Rule 16: Cite Authoritative Sources

Always use a "source" as an authority before giving a statement.

**Example:** "According to the World Health Organization, regular exercise can reduce the risk of chronic diseases."

**Explanation:** Citing authoritative sources strengthens credibility and validates statements, making the content more trustworthy.

### Boost Content Quality by Backing Claims with Proof

If you want to improve your content quality, one of the most effective strategies is to support your claims or declarations with credible sources.

**Example — Basic claim vs backed claim:**

Basic: "Depression is a mood disorder that causes persistent feelings of sadness and loss of interest."

Backed: "According to the American Psychiatric Association's Diagnostic Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, depression is a mood disorder that causes persistent feelings of sadness and loss of interest."

The second one adds credibility, trust, and contextual depth to your content.

### Build Stronger Entity Relationships

Mentioning relevant researchers, experts, or authoritative organizations in your content helps:
- Strengthen the semantic connection with trusted entities
- Boost your content's authority in the eyes of search engines
- Increase reader trust and content validation

### Long-Term Benefits

When readers trust your content, it leads to:
- Better engagement
- Positive user signals
- Stronger historical data (which search engines consider for rankings)

All of this contributes to a higher quality score for your page — and ultimately, better SEO performance.

**Final Thought:** Don't just say it — prove it. The more well-supported your content is, the more valuable it becomes for both users and search engines.

## Rule 17: Consistent Declarations Across Webpages

Always give consistent declarations across webpages.

**Example:** If one page says "coffee reduces stress," do not say "coffee increases stress" on another page.

**Explanation:** Maintain a consistent stance on each topic to build credibility and trust.

---

## Rule 18: Consistent Style Across Documents

Use a consistent style across documents.

**Example:** If your brand's tone is friendly and informative, use this style on all pages, including social media and emails.

**Explanation:** Consistent tone across platforms builds brand identity and enhances reader recognition.

---

## Rule 19: Consistent Part of Speech in Listings

Use the same Part of Speech Tag (Word Role) in the first word of the sentence for a listing.

**Verb + Noun structure:**
- Ensure proper alignment.
- Clear unnecessary clutter.
- Spend time reviewing details.
- Absorb useful information.

**Noun structure:**
- Coffee provides energy and antioxidants.
- Exercise boosts metabolism and strengthens muscles.
- Hydration improves skin and aids digestion.

Using a consistent part of speech, especially at the beginning of list items, makes the list easier to follow and more visually appealing. It also reinforces a structured, intentional tone, which is helpful for both readability and SEO.

---

## Rule 20: Content Length Rules

- Content should be as short as possible.
- Content should be as long as necessary.

Tell the same thing with lesser words but include the necessary entities and their connections in a descriptive and unique way. If a sentence is too long, the dependency tree also will be long, and the meaning between the word connections will be diluted. Google also talks about using a "query language" and "document language" ranking for matching the highest possibility.
