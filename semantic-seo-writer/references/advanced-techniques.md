## Rule 46: Parts of Speech

Parts of speech are categories that describe how words function in a sentence. Some common parts of speech are:

- **Noun:** A person, place, thing, or idea (e.g., dog, school, happiness)
- **Verb:** An action word (e.g., run, eat, think)
- **Adjective:** Describes a noun (e.g., happy, blue, quick)
- **Adverb:** Describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb (e.g., quickly, very, well)

**Example:** "The cat quickly jumped over the tall fence."

Breakdown:
- **Noun:** "cat" (the subject of the sentence, a person, place, thing, or idea)
- **Verb:** "jumped" (the action being performed)
- **Adverb:** "quickly" (describes how the action of jumping was performed)
- **Adjective:** "tall" (describes the noun "fence" by giving more information about its height)

## Rule 47: Add Perspective Richness for Better User Satisfaction

If your content contains headings or questions that can be searched by different types of users, then it's always a great idea to add perspective richness into the content.

**Example:**

How to Teach a Child to Speak English? This question can be asked by:
- A parent
- A teacher
- A relative of the child

That means the perspective is diverse, so adding different perspectives would help better satisfy various users searching for this question.

### Structuring the Answer

1. First, include facts and figures based on consensus
2. Then, add multiple perspectives for better perspective richness

### Maintain Balance

Try to ensure there's a balance in the coverage for each perspective under that heading. Giving more weight to one perspective may:
- Negatively impact the prominence of other headings
- Shift the overall content angle unintentionally

### Why Perspective Richness Matters

It's helpful for users. That's why Google loves it. This is also evident from the recent positive growth of platforms like Reddit and Quora. These platforms are thriving because their webpages are enriched with diverse perspectives.

**Final Thought:** A semantic content strategy can't be effective without: Thorough research of target users. A deep understanding of how users' intent will be satisfied throughout the content.

## Rule 48: Put "If" Statements in the Second Part of the Sentence

"If A becomes B, do X." — Wrong order

"Do X, if A becomes B." — Correct order

State what to do first, then explain the condition.

**Examples:**
- Wrong: "If it rains, take an umbrella."
- Correct: "Take an umbrella, if it rains."

## Rule 54: Ontology and Taxonomy

In the world of data organization, ontology and taxonomy help us understand how entities connect and relate to one another.

### Ontology Concept

Things or entities are connected through their shared attributes, which form the basis of an ontology.

**Cars and Trucks (Vehicles):**
- Shared Attribute: Number of Wheels
- Cars and trucks are linked by the shared attribute of having wheels.

**Apples and Oranges (Fruits):**
- Shared Attribute: Category (Fruit)
- Apples and oranges both belong to the fruit category.

### Taxonomy

Taxonomy is the process of sorting items from the most general group to more specific groups.

**Hierarchy Example:** Library > Fiction > Fantasy > Epic Fantasy

A library organizes books by broad genres such as Fiction, which can be further divided.

## Rule 55: Long-Form vs Short-Form Question Answering

"You should try to use short questions in your content." Search engines are quite good at understanding short questions in the content.

First of all the word count doesn't define if the question is short form or long form. The concepts in the questions will define it.

**A long question might look like:**

Question + Condition/declaration: "What is the importance of life if we all die?"
- "What is the importance" — Question
- "If we all die" — Condition

"What is the effect of the moon on earth's orbit despite it way being smaller compared to earth?"
- "What is the effect of the moon on earth's orbit" — Question
- "despite it way being smaller compared to earth?" — Declaration

Try to use shorter questions because longer questions might be confusing for search engines.

**If you still want to use longer questions, try to use in the following way:**
- H2: Grouper question — It should be short form
- H3: Put your questions, qualifiers (like "if"), statements, declarations, in the form of short form questions as a connected question.

## Rule 61: Converting Headings to Questions

It's always a great idea to convert your headings into questions while writing content.

### Why This Matters

Search engines generate questions because:
- It helps with information retrieval (query-dependent ranking)
- It supports information extraction
- Aligning the question format with the answer format can be extremely helpful for: Making question-answer pairs. Achieving better information responsiveness.

**Pro Tip:** If the question in your content is the same as the one generated by the search engine and gets ranked at the top as a candidate question, it allows search engines to directly learn that topic from your content.

### Example Headings

- Before: "Cups of coffee a day"
- After: "How Many Cups of Coffee a Day Are Safe?"

The second heading is: Clearer. More precise. Easier to align with an answer format.

From a user's point of view, the second one is also: More targeted. More helpful for a specific user group searching for that information.

**Personal Practice:** That's why I always prefer using questions in my semantic content briefs, along with guidelines on aligning the format of both questions and answers.

**Final Takeaway:** Using question-based headings: Helps search engines understand content better. Enhances user intent satisfaction.

## Rule 62: Truth Ranges

Truth ranges refer to the highest and lowest values identified for a specific piece of data, such as numeric values like pH levels, temperature, or speed, etc. These ranges are created when there are multiple sources providing different values for the same entity-attribute-value (E-A-V) structure, which represents a piece of information (like a substance and its pH level).

### Purpose

Truth ranges help search engines or systems deal with inconsistencies across different sources. Instead of selecting just one value, the system identifies the range of values provided by different sources and can present the most accurate and comprehensive answer.

### How it Works

If there are 90 web sources that mention the specific alchemy formula, and only 37 of them involve something related to pH level, the search engine has to filter the sources based on their attribute involvement. 37 different web sources for pH level might give 37 different pH level suggestions.

In these types of situations, search engines can use their own Knowledge Bases that are fed with Data Commons, Freebase, or manual Knowledge Base improvements, or they can create a truth range with various corners.

When the data from different sources does not align, truth ranges come into play:

1. **Collecting Values:** The search engine gathers all possible values for a particular query.
2. **Finding the Range:** After collecting values, the system identifies the minimum and maximum points. This process allows the creation of a truth range that encompasses all the possible data points.
3. **Boosting Rankings:** If no single consensus is available, a source that provides a range of values (e.g., "The pH level of X varies between Y and Z") can be favored in rankings, as it helps users understand the variability in the data.

### Example: pH Levels of a Substance

Suppose there are 37 sources giving different pH levels for a chemical:
- One source says the pH level is between 5 and 6.
- Another source claims it's between 4 and 5.
- A third source suggests it's between 6 and 7.

In this case, instead of choosing one source, the search engine identifies the truth range from 4 to 7 as the full spectrum of possible values. By using this range, the search engine provides a more accurate representation of the possible pH levels, even if no single source agrees on the exact value.

### Example: Melting Point of a Metal

Imagine you're researching the melting point of a specific metal, such as aluminum, and you come across several different sources that provide slightly varying information:
- Source A: states that the melting point of aluminum is between 659 degrees Celsius and 660 degrees Celsius.
- Source B: claims the melting point is between 660 degrees Celsius and 661 degrees Celsius.
- Source C: mentions a broader range, stating the melting point of aluminum is between 657 degrees Celsius and 662 degrees Celsius.

In this case, rather than selecting a single value (which could vary depending on factors like purity, atmospheric conditions, or measurement error), the truth range would be established as 657 degrees Celsius to 662 degrees Celsius. This range incorporates the data from all sources and gives a more comprehensive answer.

## Rule 64: Always Use the Abbreviation in Parentheses on First Mention

**Explanation:** When you introduce an entity or concept for the first time, include its abbreviation in parentheses right after the full term. This way, readers immediately know what the abbreviation stands for, making it easier to follow the content if the abbreviation is used later.

**Examples:**
- Incorrect: "Bitcoin is the main cryptocurrency asset."
- Correct: "Bitcoin (BTC) is the main cryptocurrency asset for crypto trading platforms."

**Why It Matters:** This approach improves clarity, especially in technical or professional writing, where abbreviations are frequently used. It ensures that readers don't have to guess the meaning of abbreviations later in the text.

## Rule 65: Diverse Measurement Units

If you're mentioning measurement units in your content, always try to mention more and diverse measurement units.

**Example with less measurement units:**
"For each pound lost, drink around 16-20 oz of water."

**Same sentence with more measurement units:**
"For each pound (0.45 kg) lost, drink around 16-20 oz (0.5-0.6 liters) of water."

The second sentence is more enriched with information and has more connection to different types of related entities, i.e., kg and liters.

From the user experience point of view, the second sentence is more helpful for users as well, because different users process information with different mindsets.

Thus, more units help to satisfy a more diverse and relevant target user class.

## Rule 68: Use Shorter Sentences

How Using Short Sentences in Your Content Might Help It Rank Higher in Google? The balance between words and information in content is crucial.

Using complex, long, and exhausting sentences not only makes your content boring for readers but also makes it harder for search engines to understand and rank higher.

But how does using short sentences in content make sense from a technical standpoint? The answer is "Dependency Tree."

The Dependency Tree is the interpretation of every word in a sentence as an acyclic graph.

- There is a semantic relationship between each word used in a sentence.
- The context and meaning of a word change if its position in a sentence is changed.
- Too long sentences create a long dependency tree, making word connections less clear and meaning diluted.

**The goal?** Give one "piece of information" per sentence.

**The Takeaway:**
- Answer one question clearly in a single sentence.
- Don't answer many questions vaguely in one sentence.

## Rule 69: Sentence Structure and Dependency Trees

Why Sentence Structure Matters in Content Quality. Using the right sentence structure is crucial when aiming for high-quality content.

A long and complex sentence structure may result in what's called a long dependency tree — which can hurt readability and content clarity.

### What's a Dependency Tree?

A dependency tree is how a search engine (and even the human brain) interprets the structure of a sentence — like an acyclic graph, where each word connects to another in a specific relationship. The longer the dependency tree, the greater the chances of the meaning getting diluted between those connected words.

### Comparing Two Sentences

**Sentence 1:** Apples grow in the garden.

**Sentence 2:** Apples, ripe and red, grow abundantly in the garden, which has been tended with care by my grandfather for decades, becoming a sanctuary for birds and insects.

The difference is clear: Sentence 1 delivers a single, direct message — simple and clear. Sentence 2, though rich, becomes overloaded with information, making it harder for both readers and search engines to focus on the main point.

### What's the Risk?
- Dilution of context
- Cognitive overload for readers
- Reduced clarity for search engines

Even if your intentions are to add more value, complexity without clarity can reduce overall content quality.

**Pro Tip:** One idea per sentence — keep it crisp, clear, and easy to digest. This improves both user experience and SEO performance.

**Final Thought:** Good writing isn't about how many ideas you can stuff into a sentence — it's about how clearly you can communicate one idea at a time.

## Rule 71: Prove Real Expertise with Vocabulary Richness

The number of unique words in a document isn't just a writing metric — it's a mirror of your domain expertise. That's why, 2 years ago, we built the Vocabulary Richness Auditor GPT Agent.

Unique words reflect knowledge-domain terms — the language that defines your topic, context, and authority. Using vague phrases like "to do that" won't help. Instead, verbs like "cure, treat," or "convert, transcribe" boost contextual relevance — especially for LLMs.

This principle is also behind the "0 Search Volume Keywords" concept introduced 5 years ago. Just because no one searches "fatigue" alongside "cancer" doesn't mean it lacks relevance. Fatigue, fainting, coughing, low pulse — these are semantically tied attributes, strengthening the connection to "cancer symptoms."

### Algorithmic Authorship Rules

In our model, we apply rules like:
- Use 3 examples for every plural noun: "obstacles (lack of funding, resistance from leadership, time constraints)"
- Use "or" for synonym groups: "convert or transform", not "convert (transform)"

This structure improves your n-gram profile, which is essential for how LLMs and semantic engines interpret your content.

### Headword Clusters in Action

LLMs group words like:
- thirsty → thirst
- heal → healing
- urinate → urination

These are what we call "headword clusters" in Koray's Framework. Depending on the query or answer format, either the verb or noun version is surfaced to strengthen topical alignment.
