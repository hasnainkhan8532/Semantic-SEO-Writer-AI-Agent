## Rule 21: Boolean Questions Format

Boolean Questions should start with Yes or No.

**Example:**

Does Water In Food Help When Drinking Water Is Restricted?

Yes, eating food high in water content that is easily digestible helps with overall fluid intake when drinking water is restricted.

According to Wiseman, digesting fat is the hardest and requires a lot of water.

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) recommends not eating salty foods as they can increase thirst.

## Rule 24: Optimize Discourse Integration

Discourse integration refers to the logical and contextual connection between sentences and sections of content to create a coherent flow of ideas. In the context of SEO and content creation, it involves structuring information so that search engines and users can easily understand and navigate it.

### Strategies to Optimize Discourse Integration

- **Use Clear Topic Sentences:** Begin paragraphs with sentences that summarize the main idea, guiding readers through the content.
- **Employ Transitional Phrases:** Utilize words like "therefore," "however," and "for example" to connect ideas smoothly.
- **Maintain Consistent Terminology:** Use the same terms for key concepts to avoid confusion.
- **Align Content with User Intent:** Ensure the information addresses the specific needs and questions of your target audience.

### Examples

**Without Optimized Discourse Integration:** "John went to the store. He bought some milk. The sun was shining brightly. The store was crowded. He decided to take a different route home."

**With Optimized Discourse Integration:** "John went to the store to buy some milk. Noticing the store was crowded, he decided to take a different route home."

## Rule 25: Adding Perspective Richness to the Paragraph Under Heading

First, give an overall factual answer to the question. Then, you can add the perspectives after that to increase the "perspective richness" of the content. The perspective could be diversified as well.

**Example:**

Heading: How to learn English Fast?

Paragraph: There are x ways of learning English faster. First... Second... Third... To teach a child (teacher, parent, aunt). In the above example, the perspective would be richer based on teaching children from the perspective of a teacher, parent, and aunt.

**Important:** Please don't overdo the perspectives in the above paragraph. If you overdo, you'll break your semantic structure and the prominence of other sections of the article.

You can prioritize the use of perspectives based on two factors:
- Audience and their search behavior.
- Annotations in the queries. (In simple terms, analyzing from how people are searching the queries in Google, and with what perspective we can fulfill their intent.)

## Rule 27: Focus on Entities, Not Keywords

Focus plays a crucial role in achieving a set goal for content writing. It should always be on what truly matters.

### Why Focus on Entities Instead of Keywords?

Since the introduction of Google Knowledge Graph and Hummingbird, Google has evolved to understand human language through Natural Language Processing (NLP). One key aspect of NLP is Named Entity Recognition (NER) — the ability to recognize entities in content.

### The Goal of Entity Recognition:

- Understanding what the content is about.
- Identifying the context of the content.
- Determining the depth of information provided.

### How Entities Work in Content

Entities within a piece of content are linked based on their attributes and specific context. The way these entities connect also helps Google determine the expertise of the publisher.

- Strong Entity Connections = Demonstrates Subject Matter Expertise
- Weak or Irrelevant Connections = Signals Incompetence

### What Should Be the Focus Instead of Keywords?

Instead of simply inserting keywords, the goal should be: Association of all relevant entities with detailed information, attributes, and facts within a specific context.

## Rule 28: Entity Stuffing

Entity stuffing on a webpage is not different from keyword stuffing. That's why the focus should be on attributes of the main entity, instead of stuffing a webpage with entities of different types.

An entity may have a huge list of attributes. And to deal with that long list there should be a prioritization criteria. That criteria will help identify the most important attributes for processing on a webpage.

### Attribute Filtering Criteria

**Attribute Prominence:** An attribute is considered prominent for an entity if the entity cannot be defined or exist without it. For example, the entity "coffee" cannot exist without its following attributes: Origin, Taste, Types. Covering those attributes will be important for a source to become the topical authority state in the selected knowledge domain.

**Attribute Relatedness:** This is a measure of how much an attribute is related to the source context. Source context is the website purpose and the way a website monetizes its traffic. In the Entity + Attribute pair [Coffee Filter], the filter attribute will be more related to a source selling coffee filters than a source selling coffee beans.

**Attribute Popularity:** It'll be calculated by analyzing the search demand that an attribute has in the targeted region. An attribute can have traffic potential but if it's not prominent or relevant, processing it on the source wouldn't make the sense. Because traffic is not the ultimate goal of topical authority. The ultimate goal is leads and revenue. There might be instances where the data from queries doesn't properly match with the selected attribute of an entity. The Solution? Measuring the relatedness of an attribute with query patterns and synonyms in queries.

**Final Note:** Whatever the attributes of an entity you chose to process, the goal should be to prioritize the most important attributes in the document for better contextual flow throughout the article while differentiating the macro and micro contexts from each other.

## Rule 29: Expand the Evidence with Variations

Expanding evidence means incorporating different types of supporting information to strengthen an argument and make it more persuasive. Instead of relying on a single source or type of proof, various facts, statistics, and perspectives should be included.

### Types of Supporting Evidence

- **Statistical Evidence:** Using numerical data from credible sources to support a claim. Example: "According to XYZ research, drinking coffee reduces the risk of Alzheimer's by 20%."
- **Anecdotal Evidence:** Including personal stories or user experiences that provide relatable insights. Example: "A survey by XYZ found that coffee drinkers report feeling 30% less fatigue on average."
- **Scientific Studies:** Referring to peer-reviewed research or expert findings to validate a point. Example: "Studies indicate that caffeine improves cognitive function and alertness."
- **Expert Opinions:** Citing professionals or industry leaders to add authority to an argument. Example: "Dr. John Doe, a leading neurologist, states that moderate coffee consumption supports brain health."

### Why Using a Variety of Evidence Matters

Using multiple types of evidence strengthens an argument by presenting different angles of the topic. It caters to diverse audiences who may trust data, personal experiences, or expert insights differently. This approach not only reinforces credibility but also makes the argument more engaging and compelling.

## Rule 30: Factual Sentence Structures

Factual sentences present information objectively, based on verifiable data, events, or truths. These statements can be proven true or false, avoiding opinions, beliefs, or feelings. Clear and direct wording enhances comprehension and eliminates ambiguity.

### Examples of Factual Sentences

- "Vitamin C boosts immunity." — A direct statement based on scientific evidence.
- "The Earth orbits the Sun." — A scientifically proven fact.
- "Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level." — A verifiable fact under standard conditions.
- "Barack Obama was elected as the 44th president of the United States." — A historical fact.

### Characteristics of Factual Sentences

- **Objective:** They avoid personal bias or subjective interpretation.
- **Verifiable:** They can be proven true or false through evidence or observation.
- **Clear and Direct:** They present information without embellishments or opinions.

## Rule 31: Content Originality is Not Rephrasing Words

Content originality plays a critical role in both content quality and in overcoming the quality threshold for your targeted queries.

But here's the truth: You can't measure true originality with plagiarism checkers or AI content detectors.

### Why "Fake Originality" is Dangerous

A sentence can be restructured in hundreds of ways just by changing word sequences — and that's exactly how people game content auditing tools.

But here's the catch: A content piece is not original just because it looks different on the surface. If it simply rephrases existing information, it's fake originality — and Google can detect that. And worse, it can do more harm than good.

### What Makes Content Truly Original?

To create genuinely original content, you must:
- Have it written by an actual expert on the topic, not just a generic rewriter
- Include unique contextual terms, answer terms, n-grams, and declarations
- Ensure everything is logically unified and semantically sound within the content

This not only adds value to the reader, but also justifies the computational cost search engines invest in crawling and ranking your content.

### Time = Money (For Everyone)

Let's face it — no one wants to waste time or budget on content that's simply reworded fluff to pass a tool's detection. If your content isn't genuinely adding value, it's not worth the reader's time — or Google's attention.

**Final Thought:** The more authentic and original your content is — while still aligning with consensus and factual accuracy — the higher the chances of it passing the quality threshold and earning a strong ranking position in SERPs.

## Rule 34: Sentence Structure — Subject, Predicate & Object

A sentence is divided into three parts: Subject, Predicate, and Object.

- **Subject:** The subject of a sentence is a person, place, thing, or idea. It is always doing or being something.
- **Predicate:** The predicate provides information about the subject. It includes the verb and any other words that modify or describe the action.
- **Object:** The object is the person or thing upon whom or upon which the action of the verb is carried out.

### Writing Using Semantic Triples

Instead of writing a plain sentence, you can structure your information using semantic triples.

**Example:**
- Before: "Harry Potter is a popular book series written by J.K. Rowling."
- After (using a semantic triple): "Harry Potter written by J.K. Rowling is a popular book series."

Using semantic triples to win a Featured Snippet will enhance your chances of success.

### How It Works

- Before: "The Eiffel Tower is located in Paris and was designed by Gustave Eiffel."
- After: "The Eiffel Tower, designed by Gustave Eiffel, is located in Paris."

**Breakdown of the Revised Sentence:**
- **Main Thing:** "The Eiffel Tower" — What we are talking about.
- **Special Fact About It:** "designed by Gustave Eiffel" — An important detail or feature.
- **Main Information:** "is located in Paris" — The most important or final piece of information.

This method connects important details directly to the main topic, making the sentence clearer and more focused.

## Rule 35: Types of Content on a Webpage

### Main Content
- **Description:** The core information directly related to the macro context.
- **Focus:** Should constitute the majority of the webpage.
- **Example:** Detailed descriptions, lists, tables, and comparisons about the safest cities in Germany.

### Supplementary Content
- **Description:** Additional information that supports or complements the main content.
- **Focus:** Should be related to the macro context but explores different facets or perspectives.
- **Example:** Information about the most crowded or high-crime cities in Germany as a contrast to safety-focused content.

### Ads Content
- **Description:** Advertising content strategically placed to generate revenue while complementing the overall theme.
- **Focus:** Should align with the page's context and user interests without overwhelming the main content.
- **Example:** Banner ads, sponsored links, or promotional content relevant to the topic.

## Rule 36: Key Terms Connect to Title and H1

Key terms used must connect to the title and heading 1, and in turn, connect to the topical map.

**Key Terms:** These are the main topics or keywords that your content is focused on. For example, if you're writing about "home workouts," your key terms might include "fitness," "exercise routines," "strength training," etc.

**Title:** The title of the page or article should reflect the main subject of the content. It's usually what grabs the reader's attention and also what search engines will use to determine what the page is about.

**Heading 1 (H1):** This is typically the main heading of the page or article and often mirrors or is closely related to the title. It's one of the most important on-page SEO factors.

**Connection:** The key terms should appear in both the title and H1. This connection ensures that the content is focused on a clear topic, making it easier for both search engines and users to understand.

**Example:**
- Title: "Effective Home Workouts for Beginners"
- Heading 1: "Top 10 Home Workouts for Beginners"
- Key Terms: "Home workouts," "fitness," "beginners," "exercise"

## Rule 39: Do Not Break the Information Graph with Complicated Transitions

Follow a logical order in the declarations. Imagine an "information graph" as the pathway you're leading your readers down. If the path is straightforward, readers can easily follow along and understand your message. However, if you suddenly jump between unrelated ideas or use complex language to transition between points, readers might get lost or struggle to keep up. To prevent this, use simple transitions and make sure each point builds on the previous one logically.

### Example with Good Flow

Let's say you're writing about the benefits of exercise. You might organize it like this:
1. **Start with Basic Benefits:** Begin by explaining the general health benefits, like improved cardiovascular health and stronger muscles.
2. **Move to Mental Health Benefits:** Next, discuss how exercise can improve mood and reduce stress.
3. **Conclude with Practical Tips:** Finally, provide tips on how readers can start incorporating exercise into their routine.
4. **Transition Between Points:** Use simple phrases like "In addition to physical benefits," or "Along with these physical effects, exercise also..." to smoothly move from one point to the next.

### Example with Complicated Transitions (What to Avoid)

If you discuss the benefits of exercise, then suddenly jump to explaining complex scientific terms, and then back to mental health benefits, readers might get confused.

## Rule 41: Match Tenses and Modality Between Questions and Answers

It's all about matching the tenses and modality of the answer with the question asked in the content.

### The Golden Rule

- If a question is in Present Indefinite tense, the answer should also be in Present Indefinite tense.
- If a question contains a modal verb (e.g., can, may, will, would, could), the answer should contain the same modal verb.

### Q/A Examples

**1. Present Indefinite Tense**
- Q: How does internal linking impact SEO?
- Correct A: Internal linking improves SEO by enhancing crawlability and distributing link equity.
- Wrong A: Internal linking has improved SEO by enhancing crawlability. (Incorrect tense)

**2. Past Indefinite Tense**
- Q: How did Google update its ranking algorithm in 2023?
- Correct A: Google updated its ranking algorithm in 2023 by incorporating AI-driven search enhancements.
- Wrong A: Google updates its ranking algorithm. (Incorrect tense)

**3. Future Tense**
- Q: How will AI shape the future of content marketing?
- Correct A: AI will shape the future of content marketing by generating more personalized experiences.
- Wrong A: AI shapes the future of content marketing. (Mismatch in tense)

**4. Matching Modal Verbs**
- Q: How can structured data improve search rankings?
- Correct A: Structured data can improve search rankings by helping search engines understand content better.
- Wrong A: Structured data improves search rankings. (Modal verb "can" is missing)

- Q: What would happen if a website ignored technical SEO?
- Correct A: If a website ignored technical SEO, it would struggle to rank effectively in search results.
- Wrong A: If a website ignored technical SEO, it will struggle to rank. (Mismatch in modality)
